The Case for Divisions: Restoring Structure to the BSL

The league's abandonment of divisional structure in favor of organizational efficiency has inadvertently eroded the competitive scaffolding that once elevated regular-season basketball into something consequential.

BSL Christofu 1990 Season · Commissioner's Poll

The Breakstarter League is facing a straightforward question: should divisions return? For anyone who remembers BSL 1.0, the answer is obvious. Divisions weren't just an organizational tool, they were what made the regular season matter. They created rivalries, gave February games actual stakes, and turned the long grind of the season into something worth following. When the league expanded to thirty-two teams and scrapped divisions entirely, citing seeding problems from the old six-division setup, they threw out the entire system instead of fixing what was broken. It's time to bring divisions back, and the proposed eight-division structure does exactly that while solving the issues that plagued the original format.

Here's what makes the new proposal work: eight divisions of four teams each, split evenly between the East and West. Division winners get guaranteed top-four playoff seeds, but they're slotted by overall record within that tier. Yes, this means a 45-win division champion could theoretically seed higher than a 52-win wild card. And that's the point. The system isn't designed to reward pure win totals it is designed to reward teams that dominate their rivals consistently over the course of a season. If you finish second in your division with 52 wins, you had the same opportunity to win that division and didn't. That failure has consequences, and it should.

Why Rivalries Matter

Right now, the Lakers play the Clippers four times a year. They also play the Utah Jazz four times a year. On paper, these matchups are worth the same two points in the standings. In reality, that's absurd. Lakers-Clippers isn't just another game — it's a battle for Los Angeles, for bragging rights, for which franchise gets to claim the city as their own (And which Australian GM is the best). Every possession matters because the stakes extend beyond the scoreboard. Lakers-Jazz? It's a professionally contested game between two good teams, but it lacks that edge. There's no territorial claim at stake, no civic pride on the line. It's just basketball.

The current system treats all games as equal. They're not, and pretending otherwise diminishes what makes the regular season compelling.

My proposed Pacific Division; Lakers, Clippers, Warriors, and Kings formalizes what already exists. These teams are rivals whether the league acknowledges it or not. Grouping them together means every intra-divisional game carries extra weight. A February Lakers-Clippers matchup becomes more than a regular-season contest; it's a critical game in the divisional race with direct playoff implications. This isn't manufactured drama. It's recognizing the competitive reality that history create intensity, and building a structure that amplifies it.

The same principle applies across the league. Put Leflop, Cody, Ted, and Clutch in the Central Division and you've created a regional battleground where every game matters for divisional standing. A Southwest Division with Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, and Memphis turns four Texas-adjacent teams into natural adversaries fighting for regional supremacy. These groupings aren't arbitrary  they're geographic clusters that make sense, and they give the regular season the narrative momentum it's currently missing.

The Seeding Argument

The main complaint about divisions is always the same: weak divisions will send mediocre teams to the playoffs while strong divisions cannibalize themselves. Critics point to scenarios where a sub-.500 division winner could make the playoffs ahead of a better wild-card team. To those critics: look at the NFL. In 2025, Carolina won the NFC South with an 8-9 record and hosted a 12-5 Rams team in the Wild Card round. Some people complained. Most people didn't care. Carolina earned that spot by winning their division, and they defended their home field to the final snap. That's not a bug in the system.

Division titles aren't participation trophies. They're achievements that require beating the teams that know you best. If a 52-win team finishes second in their division while a 45-win team wins theirs, the 52-win team had the same opportunity and didn't capitalize. That matters. And under the proposed system, where division winners are slotted by record within the top-four seeds, the worst seeding inequities are prevented anyway.

More importantly, divisions create competitive balance by giving meaning to games throughout the season. A team sitting at 35-30 in mid-March with no shot at a top-three seed has nothing to play for under the current format. That same team, one game back in the division race, suddenly has every remaining game mattering. Division titles become real accomplishments worth chasing, and the regular season stays competitive deep into the schedule. That's good for the league, good for fans, and good for the product on the court.

Learning from BSL 1.0

The original Breakstarter League had six divisions with five teams each. It wasn't perfect , there were legitimate complaints about teams gaming divisional matchups. But when the league expanded and scrapped divisions wholesale, something important was lost. The regular season flattened out. January and February games, once charged with divisional implications, became indistinguishable. The slow build toward playoff intensity disappeared, replaced by a formless stretch where every game carried the same weight, which meant none of them carried enough weight.

Basketball isn't just a series of isolated games. It's a narrative, and narratives need structure. Divisions provide that structure. They turn the Lakers-Clippers rivalry into a season long story instead of four disconnected games. They make a late-February matchup between division rivals feel like it matters because it does for seeding, for pride, for the right to claim regional superiority. The current format can't replicate that, no matter how clean the administrative structure looks on paper.

The scheduling implications are straightforward: teams would face divisional opponents more frequently, with the remainder of the eighty-two-game schedule filled through cross-divisional and inter-conference matchups. The result is a league structure where geography matters, where proximity breeds intensity, and where the regular season functions as something more than mere playoff preamble.

Why This Matters

The commissioner's poll is still open, and there are legitimate questions to answer. Scheduling will be more complex. There's always a risk of unintended consequences. Some divisions might be stronger than others in a given year. These concerns are real, but they're solvable through careful planning and a willingness to adjust the system as needed.

What can't be solved is the current format's fundamental problem: it makes the regular season less interesting than it should be. The BSL has great players, compelling storylines, and passionate fans. The organizational structure should amplify those strengths, not diminish them. Divisions do that. They make Lakers-Clippers games in February feel like playoff games. They turn Central Division matchups into regional wars. They give late-season games meaning they wouldn't otherwise have. Most importantly, they acknowledge what the flat conference model refuses to admit: basketball isn't an abstract competition between identical franchises. It's territorial, emotional, and rooted in geography and history. Proximity and rivalries matter as much as talent and strategy.

BSL 1.0 understood that, even if the execution wasn't perfect. The current proposal fixes those flaws while keeping what made divisions work in the first place. The league should embrace it. Fans deserve a regular season that matters from October through April, not just a six-month preamble to the playoffs. Divisions make that possible.
- BSL Christofu
